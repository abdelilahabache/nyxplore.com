Statue of Liberty

A universal symbol of freedom and democracy

Lady Liberty

The Statue of Liberty is a colossal neoclassical sculpture on Liberty Island in New York Harbor. Designed by Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi and built by Gustave Eiffel, the statue was dedicated on October 28, 1886, as a gift from France to the United States.

The Statue of Liberty was designated as a National Monument in 1924 and a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1984. It remains one of the most recognizable symbols of the United States and has welcomed millions of immigrants arriving by ship to New York Harbor.

Fascinating Facts

Construction

The statue was constructed in France, shipped overseas in crates, and assembled on the completed pedestal on what was then called Bedloe's Island. The copper statue was built using the repoussé technique.

Symbolism

The seven spikes on the crown represent the seven oceans and seven continents, indicating universal liberty. The tablet in her left hand reads "July 4, 1776" in Roman numerals, the date of American independence.

Restoration

For its centennial in 1986, the statue underwent a major restoration that included replacing the torch (now covered in gold leaf) and strengthening the internal structure designed by Gustave Eiffel.

Visitor Information

Getting There

The only way to access Liberty Island is by ferry. Statue Cruises is the official ferry service provider and departs from:

  • Battery Park in Manhattan
  • Liberty State Park in New Jersey

Ferry details:

  • Ferries run daily (except December 25)
  • First ferry departs at 8:30am
  • Last ferry departs at 3:30pm
  • Security screening required

Ticket Options

There are several ticket options available depending on what you'd like to experience:

  • Grounds Ticket: Access to Liberty Island and Ellis Island
  • Pedestal Ticket: Includes access to the pedestal observation deck
  • Crown Ticket: Limited access to the crown (must climb 354 steps)
  • Hard Hat Tour: Guided tour of Ellis Island Hospital

Note: Crown access requires advanced reservation and involves climbing narrow stairs.

Historical Timeline

1865

French historian Édouard de Laboulaye proposes the idea of a monument to commemorate Franco-American friendship and liberty.

1875

Work begins in France under sculptor Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi, with structural engineering by Gustave Eiffel.

1885

Statue arrives in New York Harbor in 350 pieces packed in 214 crates. Assembly takes four months.

1886

Statue dedicated on October 28 by President Grover Cleveland with a parade through Manhattan.

1924

Designated as a National Monument, recognizing its significance to American history and culture.

1984

Declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site as a "masterpiece of the human spirit."

Visitor Tips

Best Time to Visit

Early morning ferries (8:30-10am) are least crowded. Spring and fall offer pleasant weather with smaller crowds than summer. Winter visits have the fewest visitors but check for ferry schedule changes.

What to Bring

Comfortable walking shoes, water, sunscreen, and a camera. Large bags are not permitted (maximum size 18"x16"x8"). Food options are limited on the islands so consider bringing snacks.

Accessibility

The grounds and pedestal are wheelchair accessible with elevators available. The crown is only accessible via a narrow 354-step staircase. Service animals are permitted throughout.
