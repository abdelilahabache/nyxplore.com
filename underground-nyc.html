Underground NYC: Hidden Tunnels, Secrets, and Stories

By NYxplore Team May 16, 2025 8 min read
Urban Exploration NYC History Hidden Gems Tunnels

New York City is iconic for its towering skyline and packed sidewalks, but hidden below lies a mysterious and fascinating world — the underground NYC you don’t see on a subway map. From forgotten tunnels to clandestine art and megaprojects in progress, this is the other side of the city.

The Forgotten Subway Stations

Stations like the beautifully tiled City Hall Subway Station, closed in 1945, are remnants of a different era. While not open to the public, you can glimpse it by staying on the 6 train past the final stop at Brooklyn Bridge.

Secret Tunnels & Passageways

Hidden beneath iconic landmarks like Grand Central Terminal are tunnels with colorful pasts — including Track 61, rumored to have transported President Franklin D. Roosevelt in secrecy. Stories abound of Prohibition-era bootlegger routes and Cold War bunkers below Columbia University and the Brooklyn Navy Yard.

Underground Art Scene

Beneath Riverside Park, the Freedom Tunnel features decades of layered graffiti and murals — a raw canvas where street artists like Chris “Freedom” Pape made their mark. Meanwhile, the elusive Underbelly Project transformed a sealed subway station into an invitation-only gallery — one that the public may never see.

NYC's Hidden Infrastructure

The city is supported by incredible feats of subterranean engineering. Water Tunnel No. 3 — under construction since 1970 — spans 60+ miles and helps supply millions with fresh water. East Side Access is another massive effort connecting the LIRR to Grand Central via vast new tunnels deep below Midtown.

People Living Below

For some, NYC’s tunnels aren’t just a curiosity — they’re home. Known as “mole people,” these individuals seek shelter in abandoned tunnels. While often romanticized or misunderstood, outreach programs strive to connect these residents with safer, supportive alternatives.

From hidden art to historic secrets, the underground side of New York tells a story of survival, creativity, and engineering brilliance — all quietly running beneath your feet.
