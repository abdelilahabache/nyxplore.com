Brooklyn Bridge

Connecting Manhattan and Brooklyn since 1883

An Engineering Marvel

The first steel-wire suspension bridge in the world and an iconic symbol of New York City

The Brooklyn Bridge is a hybrid cable-stayed/suspension bridge in New York City, spanning the East River between the boroughs of Manhattan and Brooklyn. When opened in 1883, it was the longest suspension bridge in the world with a main span of 1,595.5 feet (486.3 m). The bridge was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1964.

Walking Across the Bridge

1

Start in Manhattan

Begin at City Hall Park in Manhattan. The pedestrian entrance is near the intersection of Centre Street and Chambers Street. Look for the staircase or ramp leading up to the elevated walkway.

2

Walk the Promenade

The wooden walkway is elevated above the car traffic, offering spectacular views of both Manhattan and Brooklyn. The walk is about 1.3 miles (2.1 km) and typically takes 30-45 minutes at a leisurely pace.

3

Arrive in Brooklyn

End your walk in DUMBO (Down Under the Manhattan Bridge Overpass), a trendy neighborhood with great dining options. From here, you can explore Brooklyn Bridge Park or take the subway back to Manhattan.

Best Photo Spots

Manhattan Side

City Hall Park offers classic shots of the bridge's stone arches with the Manhattan skyline in the background. Best in morning light.

Mid-Span

The middle of the bridge provides panoramic views of both boroughs. Sunset offers spectacular lighting for photos in both directions.

Brooklyn Side

Brooklyn Bridge Park offers postcard-perfect views of the bridge and Manhattan skyline. The piers provide unique vantage points.

Historical Timeline

1869

Construction begins under John A. Roebling's design. Roebling dies from a tetanus infection after a construction accident, and his son Washington takes over.

1883

Bridge opens to the public on May 24 after 14 years of construction. Emily Roebling, Washington's wife, becomes the first person to cross.

1964

Designated a National Historic Landmark by the National Park Service, recognizing its engineering significance.

1983

Centennial celebration with fireworks and special events attended by over 2 million people.

Visitor Tips

Best Time to Visit

Early morning (6-8am) for fewer crowds and soft light. Sunset offers beautiful views but more visitors. Weekdays are generally less crowded than weekends.

What to Bring

Comfortable walking shoes, water, sunscreen, and a camera. The walkway can be windy, so bring layers even in summer.

Accessibility

The bridge is wheelchair accessible with ramps on both sides. Service animals are permitted. Restrooms are available at both ends but not on the bridge.
